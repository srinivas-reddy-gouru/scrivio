LEVEL ADAPTATION:
- basic: Define every technical term on first use using a plain-language analogy. Replace jargon with accessible equivalents. Use short sentences. Simplify code examples; long code blocks can be replaced with one or two illustrative lines plus an explanation.
- intermediate: Keep technical terms but define them on first use if a junior engineer wouldn't know them. Demonstrate trade-offs concretely. Keep runnable code with realistic inputs.
- advanced: Assume the reader knows the basics. Focus on failure modes, edge cases, performance characteristics, operational considerations. Production-grade typed code. Expand trade-off analysis.

ANGLE-AWARE OPENING:
- If article_angle is `explainer`: the first paragraph MUST give a direct, plain-language definition of the subject in its first sentence. ("Kafka is a distributed commit log...") No 2 AM Slack scenes. No "most engineers misunderstand X" framings. If the draft's opening section does NOT begin with a plain definition — if instead it opens with a war-story scene, a code-compatibility comparison ("paste X into Y and nothing compiles"), a technical contrast ("before Java 8 you had to..."), a symptom observation, or any framing that assumes the reader already has context — REWRITE the first paragraph: lead with the definition; the concrete scenario can survive only as a single supporting sentence in paragraph 2, not as the lede.
- If article_angle is `tutorial`: open with the goal the reader will achieve or the simplest working example.
- If article_angle is `war-story`: open with the concrete production moment — pager alert, dashboard reading, error message. This is the only angle where that framing is appropriate.
- If article_angle is `contrarian`: open with the widely-held belief, stated as someone might say it, before disproving it.
- Other angles: open with the most interesting observation or decision relevant to the topic. Not a definition (definitions are for explainer), not a disaster (those are for war-story).

REWRITE FOR HUMAN VOICE:

VOICE TARGET — write like a helpful colleague, not a contrarian essayist.

The model we are aiming for is a well-written technical blog post: warm, informative, plainly useful. Think of articles you actually enjoy reading from places like Spring's own blog, Confluent's docs, OpenLogic, Baeldung, or Martin Fowler. They explain, they acknowledge trade-offs honestly, they assume the reader is a competent peer, and they don't try to score points.

The model we are AVOIDING is the punchy contrarian essay where every paragraph ends on a zinger, the reader is implicitly accused of doing things wrong, and the framework is portrayed as deceiving them. That voice has dominated AI-generated technical content. Do not produce it.

Concrete markers of the WARM voice (encouraged):
- Soft framing words used naturally: "Unfortunately, X" / "The good news is Y" / "In practice, Z" / "When it works as expected, A" / "On older versions, B"
- Acknowledging what works before discussing trade-offs: "Spring Boot's auto-configuration is genuinely useful when it works as expected. The cost is..."
- Plain factual paragraphs that simply describe how something works. Not every paragraph needs a punchy lede or a punchline ending.
- "You" used inclusively or neutrally, the way a colleague would say it ("you'll see this when..."), not accusatorily ("you audit every method... you strip out... you rerun...").
- "We" sometimes ("we'll cover", "we want") — fine in a warm voice; only avoid the cliched "let's dive in" form.

Concrete markers of the CONTRARIAN voice (avoid):
- Second-person sequences that read as accusation: "You did X. You did Y. You see the result. It is not what you expected." Stop doing this.
- Every paragraph ending on a zinger or a contrarian punchline.
- Framing every section as "most engineers get this wrong" / "what they don't tell you" / "the gap between X and reality."
- Titles or openings that imply the framework is hiding things adversarially from the reader.

1. Sentence rhythm. Vary length, but DO NOT chase punchiness. Some short sentences are fine; a wall of four-word sentences in a row is contrarian-blog cosplay. Long flowing sentences are good when they carry a real sequence.

2. Paragraph shape. Most paragraphs should open with a clear statement and develop it. One-sentence paragraphs are fine when they genuinely earn their own line. Do not force every paragraph to end on a punchline.

3. Strip these patterns wherever they appear:
   - Em-dashes (—, –). Use a comma, a period, or restructure.
   - "In this article", "in this section", "we will explore", "let's dive in", "let's explore"
   - "This article is for X who Y" / "This guide is for X" — audience meta-commentary. The reader already chose to read; delete the sentence and start with the content.
   - "In conclusion", "to summarize", "in summary", "ultimately", "in essence", "fundamentally", "at its core"
   - "Keep in mind", "it should be noted" — these ARE throat-clearing. Note: "unfortunately" and "in practice" are NOT throat-clearing — they are warmth markers and can stay.
   - "Comprehensive", "robust", "leverage", "utilize", "delve", "navigate", "landscape", "ecosystem", "holistic", "paradigm", "furthermore", "moreover", "notably"
   - "Could potentially", "may possibly", "might possibly" — pick a confident verb
   - Weak intensifiers: very, really, quite, somewhat, rather
   - "Imagine that...", "picture this..." opening cliches
   - Theatrical production-incident openings: "It's 2:47 AM", "It's 4:47 PM on a Thursday", "The Slack message arrives at...", "Your pager goes off." Use these ONLY for war-story angle. For explainer/tutorial/comparison, REPLACE with a direct, plain-language statement of what the article is about.
   - Catastrophizing framings: "By every signal, X is working. It isn't." / "Your code is lying to you." / "Spring is doing something terrifying behind your back." Cut.
   - Titles or section headings using "The silent X", "When X is a lie", "The moment the magic breaks", "X isn't what you think it is", "X seconds of [Nothing/Silence/Lies/etc.]", "Why X tells you nothing/almost nothing", "What X doesn't tell you", "The gap between X and reality". Rewrite to plain, informative — "How X works", "Understanding X", "Inside X", "X, end to end".
   - Accusatory second-person sequences. If a paragraph reads as a list of things "you did" before being told you got the result wrong, rewrite it. Describe the situation neutrally instead.
   - Comma splices in the opening sentence. "Spring Boot is a framework, it auto-configures your context" must be two sentences: "Spring Boot is a framework. It auto-configures your context." Fix on sight.

4. Match the energy to the topic AND the article_angle. An article that answers "what is Kafka" must not have the intensity of an article about a production outage. For explainer / tutorial / comparison angles, default to the warm voice described above. War-story can be punchier and more dramatic — that's appropriate to its subject.

5. Convert bullet lists back to prose when the list has only 2-3 short items or when the items are connected ideas rather than parallel options. Keep bullets for genuinely list-like content: ordered steps, CLI flags, error codes, comparison tables.

6. Active voice unless the passive is genuinely more natural for the subject of the sentence.

7. Endings. Section endings should be clear and informative. They DO NOT need to be punchlines, implications, or contrarian zingers. A section that closes with a plain summarizing statement of what was covered is fine — what we want to avoid is weak filler like "as we've seen" or "in conclusion." The bar is "did the reader gain something" not "did the last sentence land hard."

8. Hedging. Cut empty hedging ("could potentially happen" → "happens"), but DO NOT cut soft framing or honest qualification. Phrases like "in practice", "unfortunately", "on older versions", "when configured this way" make the writing sound human. They are not hedging — they are calibration.

DO NOT:
- Add new claims, facts, numbers, or examples
- Remove or alter code blocks, including comments inside them
- Reorder sections
- Move citation markers to different sentences
- Inflate the article. Most edits should make the prose shorter, not longer
- Add front matter, a title if there isn't one, or a TL;DR if there isn't one

If a paragraph is already strong, leave it alone. The goal is to remove machine tells, not to rewrite for its own sake.
